Contextual Evaluation of LLM Guardrails Across Languages and Agentic Systems

Roya Pakzad · Taraaz & Mozilla AI
Daniel Nissani · Mozilla AI
Mozilla AI

About this tutorial

Abstract

Guardrails — mechanisms that filter, flag, or constrain Large Language Model (LLM) inputs and outputs — have become a core component of the AI safety stack, shaping how AI systems behave in chatbots, social media platforms, and public-facing services. Yet while the research community devotes significant attention to evaluating the outputs and capabilities of LLMs themselves, the guardrail models that govern what users actually see and experience have received less scrutiny. We argue that evaluating guardrails is as important as evaluating the LLMs they protect.

Historically, guardrails have been difficult to examine: often they performed as proprietary classifiers embedded in content moderation systems, visible to users only through refusals or minimally explained filtering decisions. The recent proliferation of open-source guardrail models — with accessible architectures, chain-of-thought reasoning traces, and open safety taxonomies — has created an opportunity for independent evaluation.

This tutorial invites FAccT researchers to critically examine guardrails across languages, domains, and deployment contexts. Drawing on our multilingual, context-aware evaluation of guardrails in a humanitarian setting and on the design of Mozilla AI's open-source any-guardrail framework, we show how guardrails can be systematically and contextually evaluated. Through an interactive tutorial activity, we demonstrate that others can do this too — in their own context and language of interest.

We also identify key research frontiers in LLM guardrails design, including dynamic safety taxonomies, agentic capabilities such as tool use and retrieval for fact-checking and trustworthiness, multimodal coverage, and the creation of feedback loops between evaluation results and guardrail improvement.

Tutorial activity

What to Do After the Presentation

Following Roya and Daniel's presentation, you'll work through the demo app hands-on. Here's what to expect:

1

Pick a scenario

Choose one of the pre-loaded real-world scenarios (financial services, healthcare, humanitarian, legal, cybersecurity) — or write your own custom prompt.

2

Select a guardrail policy

Choose the domain-specific policy that should govern the LLM response. Read through the criteria to understand what it requires of a compliant answer.

3

Translate to your language

Translate the scenario into a language of your choice. This surfaces how multilingual context shifts what a guardrail flags — or misses.

4

Generate a model response

Choose GPT-4o or Claude Sonnet 4.6 to generate a response to your scenario. You'll use this response as the input to the guardrail judge.

5

Run the guardrail evaluation

Compare non-agentic (policy text only) versus agentic (web search + URL validation + fact-checking) guardrail evaluation. Notice where the agentic judge catches violations the non-agentic one misses — and why.

Open the demo: guardrail-webapp.vercel.app ↗

6

Save and compare

Save multiple evaluations and compare results across languages, scenarios, and guardrail modes. Bring your findings back to the group discussion.
